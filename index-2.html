Meta · Open-Source AI Strategy

Meta is giving its most powerful
AI models away for free. Why?

To understand the strategy, you need one concept from a 2002 blog post — and one number from Meta's own balance sheet.

Chapter 01 · The concept

Commoditize your complements

In 2002, software engineer Joel Spolsky wrote one of the most useful sentences in business strategy: every product has complements — things always bought alongside it — and your profits rise when those complements get cheaper. If two products are always consumed together, making one of them free expands the market for the other while destroying the margin of anyone trying to sell the free one.

"Smart companies try to commoditize their complements. If you can get your complement to zero cost, you capture nearly all the value in the market."

— Joel Spolsky, Joel on Software, 2002

The classic example: Intel wanted to sell chips, so it helped fund free operating system development. The OS is a complement to the chip — always bought together. Make the OS free, sell more chips at higher margins. Intel made no money from the OS. It didn't need to.

Chapter 02 · The precedent

Meta already ran this play — with server racks

Around 2011, Meta was paying fat margins to hardware vendors for data center infrastructure. Its fix: publish the specs. They launched the Open Compute Project — open-sourcing billions of dollars of proprietary data center design — and invited the entire industry to adopt the standard.

2011
Meta open-sources its data center hardware designs. Vendors must now compete against a free specification.
2013
Microsoft, Goldman Sachs, and others join. The standard spreads. Hardware vendor margins collapse industry-wide.
2015+
Meta's infrastructure costs drop ~$2B/yr. Zero revenue from Open Compute. Payback entirely in cost savings — forever.

Meta spent once to create a free standard, then benefited permanently from destroyed supplier pricing power. It captured no platform. It owned nothing. It just made its inputs cheaper — and that was enough.

Chapter 03 · The replication

Llama is Open Compute for AI — and the weapon is sharper

Meta runs its own AI models internally — it is not paying OpenAI or Google for inference. The target of Llama is not Meta's own cost base. The target is its competitors' business models. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google Cloud all depend on charging for model access to survive. Llama attacks that from both directions simultaneously.

The double squeeze on competitors
Price pressure → revenue
Llama is a free substitute. Every developer who builds on it puts a ceiling on what anyone can charge for inference. As Llama improves, that ceiling drops. Competitors must match free — or lose the market.
Cost pressure → expenses
Open-sourcing forces competitors to accelerate their own training runs to stay ahead of the free benchmark. Faster releases, more compute, more researchers. Llama raises the cost floor of staying competitive.
Revenue falls. Costs rise. Margin compressed from both sides simultaneously. For a company already burning cash, this is not a nuisance — it is a path to insolvency.

Chapter 04 · The war chest

Why Meta can sustain this — and where the money comes from

The critical asymmetry: Meta does not need Llama to make money from Llama. Every dollar spent improving the model also improves its own feed ranking and ad targeting — generating advertising revenue with proven ROI entirely independent of the open-source strategy. Meta's AI spend pays for itself before the competitive weapon even fires.

But there is something else. Meta has $83.5B in cumulative Reality Labs losses on its history — and a lesson it learned the hard way about what happens when you confuse a tool for an ecosystem.

The Reality Labs context: From 2020 to 2025, Meta burned $83.5B trying to own the next computing platform — VR/AR. The logic was identical to what drove Windows and iOS: whoever owns the platform the world runs on controls the margins of everyone who builds on it. Zuckerberg believed VR was that platform. He was wrong — or at least too early. Horizon Worlds shuts down June 2026. The lesson Meta internalized: you can spend aggressively to own ecosystems and still lose.

Which raises the uncomfortable question about what comes next.

Chapter 05 · The open question

Llama is the right play — but is AI the right bet?

Open Compute is the correct analogy for Llama. Meta is not trying to own AI. It is trying to make AI cheaper — for itself, and devastatingly expensive for competitors who need to charge for it. That is a clean, proven strategy. The bull and bear cases are real but bounded.

Open Compute applied to AI: bull and bear
Bull Open Compute worked. Meta drove down infra costs ~$2B/yr by open-sourcing hardware specs. Applied to AI inference — a far larger cost base — Llama compounds. OpenAI and Anthropic, already burning cash, cannot sustain a price war against free while also accelerating training runs.
Bear Google doesn't bleed. The double squeeze works on cash-burning startups. It does not bankrupt Alphabet, which generated $132B in net income and $73B in free cash flow in 2025 — and has its own frontier models. Meta may weaken OpenAI and Anthropic while leaving its largest AI competitor untouched.
Bull The FCF machine is the moat. Meta's $46B in free cash flow and $83B in operating income means it can fund this war of attrition indefinitely. OpenAI projects $115B in cumulative losses before profitability in 2030. The profitable incumbent always wins a war of attrition.
Bear AI is a tool, not an ecosystem. Open Compute gave Meta cheaper servers — not control of the server market. Llama gives Meta cheaper inference — not control of AI. Meta wins inputs. It wins no lock-in, no developer ecosystem, no next frontier to tax. Which raises the harder question below.

Here is the question that does not have a clean answer: Meta has $46B in annual free cash flow and a freed-up $19B/yr Reality Labs budget. Pouring it into Llama is a smart way to hurt competitors and cheapen inputs. But AI, by its own nature as a tool, will not be the next ecosystem that whoever controls it gets to tax. Mark Zuckerberg knows — better than anyone — what it costs to be late to an ecosystem. So the real board question is not just how much to spend on Llama. It is whether Llama is the destination, or a holding pattern while the next true platform comes into view.

The board question

Should Meta go all-in on Llama — maximizing the double squeeze on competitors — or hold capital in reserve for the next ecosystem play? And if it does go all-in: how much damage can it actually inflict on OpenAI and Anthropic before they either adapt or die?
